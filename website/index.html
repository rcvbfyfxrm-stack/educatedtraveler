FOUNDER · I CHECK EVERY PLACE MYSELF

I left school at 18 to find out what the world was really about, and I've been crossing it by sea and by land ever since — cooking for a living, and learning from the masters I met along the way. So I know the difference between a real school and a good-looking website.

I · The beginning

So I went.

At 18, everyone expected university and a career. I wanted to learn by doing — not watch the world on a screen. So I left.

II · The kitchens

Every kitchen a classroom.

Michelin kitchens in France, street stalls in Bangkok — fifteen years cooking for a living, most of them at sea.

III · The sea

Fifty thousand nautical miles.

Learned to sail the Siam sea, raced off Sydney, crossed oceans. The sea cares about judgment, not a résumé.

IV · The depths

The deeper I went, the quieter it got.

Freediving Mexican cenotes and the open blue, sharks in the Galápagos. Fear turned into respect.

V · The heights

Strangers turned into crew.

Annapurna at 4,130 m, passes across the Himalaya. The people on the trail taught me more than any classroom.

VI · The stillness

Handed, at the source.

Breathwork in Bali, Ayurveda in India — and in the Thar desert of Rajasthan, the idea for all of this took shape.

I / VI

Nobody pays to be on the Atlas — no commission, no selling the trip. The order is the strength of the community, never the size of the wallet. And if I wouldn't send a friend, it isn't on the map.

Before you trust any school — mine or anyone's — the five questions I ask
  1. Who actually teaches it — by name, with a track record you can check?
  2. Is the craft alive in that place, or is the school the only thing there?
  3. What do you walk away with — a recognised qualification, or a certificate they printed themselves?
  4. Can you speak to someone who actually did it — not a testimonial on their own page?
  5. What happens on a bad day — weather, injury, a teacher who doesn't show?
