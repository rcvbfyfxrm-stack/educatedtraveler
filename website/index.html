The Story

I left school at 18 to find out what the world was really about.

So I went — by sea and by land, learning from the masters I met along the way. Here is some of it.

Chapter I

The Beginning

At 18, everyone expected university, career, the usual path. But I kept asking: is this the life I actually want to live? I didn't want to watch simulations of the world — I wanted to learn by doing. So I went.

Chapter II

The Kitchens

Michelin kitchens in France. Street vendors in Bangkok. Cycling through French vineyards, learning wine from the people who grow the grapes. Each kitchen was a classroom. Each chef, a teacher.

Chapter III

50,000 Nautical Miles

Learning to sail in Thailand. Regattas in Sydney. Blue-water crossings across every ocean. Private chef on some of the world's finest yachts. The ocean doesn't care about your resume. It cares about your judgment.

Chapter IV

The Depths & Heights

Freediving in Mexico. Shark diving in the Galápagos. Annapurna Base Camp at 4,130m. Fear became respect. Strangers became lifelong friends.

Chapter V

The Stillness

Breathwork in Bali. Ayurveda in India. Learning presence from masters who had given their lives to it. The body knows. You just have to listen.

Everywhere I went, the same thing was true: a real skill lives somewhere specific, in someone's hands, among the people who came to chase it — never on a screen.

So I'm building the way in — the map I wish I'd had.

Why you can trust this map

What I check before I send you anywhere

I'm Arnaud. I left school at 18 to find out what the world was really about, and I've been crossing it by sea and by land ever since — cooking for a living, and learning from the masters I met along the way. So I know the difference between a real school and a good-looking website. Nobody pays to be on the Atlas: no commission, no selling the trip. The order is the strength of the community, never the size of the wallet. Most pages I've checked by hand; the few I'm still verifying say so. And if I wouldn't send a friend, it isn't on the map.

Before you trust any school — mine or anyone else's — five questions to ask
  1. Who actually teaches it? Can you find them by name, with a track record you can check yourself?
  2. Is the craft alive in that place, or is the school the only thing there?
  3. What do you walk away with — a recognised qualification, or a certificate they printed themselves? Ask which.
  4. Can you speak to someone who did the course — a real person, not a testimonial on their own page?
  5. What happens on a bad day — weather, an injury, a teacher who doesn't show? A serious place has an honest answer.

If a place dodges these, that's your answer.
