Chapter I

The Question

I got my Bac in science at 18 and thought: now what?

Everyone expected what came next: university, career, the usual path.

I had a different question: What if I just... went?

So I did. Australia first. I landed with enough money for a few weeks and a willingness to figure it out. I found work in kitchens — not because I dreamed of being a chef, but because restaurants always need hands, and I needed to eat.

That's how it started. Not with a master plan. With curiosity and a plane ticket.

Chapter II

The Kitchens

Cooking became the thread that connected everything.

In France, I trained with chefs who'd spent their lives chasing perfection. 5 AM prep. Service that felt like battle. Standards that seemed impossible until they became natural.

In Southeast Asia, I discovered that mastery doesn't require Michelin stars. A grandmother in Bangkok making the same pad thai for 40 years. A pho vendor in Vietnam whose broth was three generations old.

On superyachts, I cooked for guests who'd eaten at every famous restaurant. The job taught me to source — dawn markets in Provence, fishermen in Greek harbors.

Each kitchen was a classroom. Each chef, a teacher. The world's cuisines became mine — not through study, but through fire and repetition.

Chapter IV

The Ocean

The dream started with books — those classic navigator stories, sailors crossing oceans alone, reading the stars.

Then I stepped onto a regatta sailboat in Sydney harbor. The heel of the boat under your feet. The crew working as one organism. The wind not as obstacle but as language.

In that moment, I knew: this is what I want.

I earned my RYA in Thailand — warm water, steady winds. At 22, I joined my first passage boat. The owner taught me everything: navigation, engineering, the patience to wait out weather.

50,000+

Nautical miles across every ocean

The ocean doesn't care about your resume. It cares about your judgment. That education can't be faked.

Chapter V

The Depths

Freediving found me in Thailand.

Not the tourist version — the real practice. Learning to descend on a single breath. Discovering that depth reveals who you are when everything else is stripped away.

The instructors taught more than technique. They taught presence. The ability to be completely where you are, with nothing but your breath and the blue.

In the Galapagos, I dove with sharks. Open water. No cage. Guided by people who'd spent decades understanding these animals.

Fear transformed into respect. Respect into something like connection.

Chapter VI

The Mountains

The Himalayas called, and I answered.

Weeks of trekking. Annapurna Base Camp at 4,130 meters. Thin air, heavy pack, views that made the suffering worth it.

India especially changed me. The chaos of Delhi. The ghats of Varanasi at sunrise. The backwaters of Kerala. The mountains of Ladakh.

The spirituality there wasn't something people performed — it was how they lived. The pace was different. Conversations went deeper. Strangers became friends in hours.

On those roads, I met photographers who taught light, trekkers who knew every plant, teachers who'd renounced everything except the joy of sharing what they knew.

Chapter VII

The Pattern

Somewhere in all those miles, I noticed something.

Every skill worth having came the same way:

1

Where the knowledge lived

2

Not YouTube videos at 2 AM. Not online courses I'd never finish. Real learning. At the source. With people who'd dedicated their lives to it.

And I realized: this path is available to anyone.
