The Atlas is the map

Lab Weeks are the map come true.

A few times a year, we take one place on the Atlas and open the door for real: one week, at the source, in a master's own workshop, with a small cohort of people chasing the same craft. You heard about one from a friend? This is the page they meant.

The standard

Every Lab Week clears the same bar.

All four, or it doesn't run:

A named master

A real person with a record you can check yourself — who actually teaches the week, every day, in person.

Their own workshop

The master's own lab, bench or kitchen — a room built for teaching, not a rented corner of somewhere else.

Open enrolment

Anyone serious can take a chair. Nothing to win, no one you need to know. The opposite of the gatekept stage.

At the source

In the place where the craft is most alive — because who you meet there matters as much as what you learn.

And one rule about the numbers: they're published from day one. Every Lab Week states its minimum out loud. If enough people take a chair, it runs; if not, it doesn't — and everyone is repaid in full. No countdown timers, no "almost gone". The honest count is the only urgency we use.

You leave with the work in your hands, a certificate that says exactly what it should — attendance and the work you presented, verified if anyone ever asks us — and the cohort itself: people worth knowing, met at the bench.

Shape the next one

What would you cross an ocean to learn?

Lab Weeks are chosen by the Circle, not by a calendar. Name the craft — the one you'd actually take a week for — and you'll be the first we write to when a master opens that door. That's the whole deal: no funnel, no noise, first word.

One email when it's real — never a drip. The Atlas already maps 99 crafts; yours is probably on it.
