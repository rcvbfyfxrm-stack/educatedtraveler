Avant-Garde & Modernist Technique

The movement whose own masters buried its most famous name — while its technique quietly took over the best kitchens on earth.

Spherification, foams, sous-vide, liquid nitrogen: real chemistry that became standard in fine dining — and a marketing phrase, "molecular gastronomy," that the very chefs it's pinned on publicly rejected.

What it actually is

Rigour that got mistaken for theatre

Strip away the smoke. Underneath is a body of technique — precise temperature, the chemistry of gels and foams, vacuum and pressure — worked out in a handful of restaurants and then absorbed into fine dining so completely that most of it is now just how good kitchens cook. Sous-vide is standard equipment. Hydrocolloids sit on every serious pastry shelf.

What it is not is the cliché — the spherified everything, the dry-ice fog, the foam on the foam. That version is a caricature the founders themselves warned against, and the honest read of this craft is learning to tell the two apart: the technique that lasted from the spectacle that dated.

The part that surprises everyone

Five things nobody tells you about elBulli

01

Two million requests a season — for about 8,000 seats. The most famous restaurant of the era turned away more than 99 in every 100 people who asked for a table.

02

It closed at its absolute peak. In July 2011, elBulli shut for good while it was still three-Michelin-starred and had just been named the world's best restaurant for the fifth time.

03

It lost money for its last decade. The restaurant ran at a loss from 2000 on — it survived on the books and the lectures, not on the dinners.

04

It shut for six months every year — to invent. Each winter the kitchen closed and the team decamped to a Barcelona workshop to do nothing but develop the next year's dishes.

05

The definitive book weighs 23.7 kilos. Nathan Myhrvold's Modernist Cuisine runs to six volumes and 2,438 pages; its first printing of 6,000 copies cost over a million dollars to produce.

Where it comes from

elBulli, and the name the masters buried

It grew up in a cove. elBulli, above Cala Montjoi on the Costa Brava, under chef Ferran Adrià, was named the world's best restaurant a record five times (2002, and 2006 through 2009) and held three Michelin stars — then closed at its peak in 2011 and became a foundation. In 2011 the field also got its reference book, Nathan Myhrvold's Modernist Cuisine, which gave it the name that stuck: modernist, not molecular.

Because "molecular gastronomy" was a label the chefs it's pinned on threw out in public. In 2006, Adrià, Heston Blumenthal, Thomas Keller and food-science writer Harold McGee co-signed a Statement on the New Cookery in The Observer:

"The term 'molecular gastronomy'… does not describe our cooking, or indeed any style of cooking." Adrià hates the term; Blumenthal calls it "elitist." The men the phrase made famous refused it. Statement on the New Cookery · The Observer, 10 December 2006

That is the spine of the whole craft, and the reason it's worth learning honestly. The technique is real and the label was always marketing — so the first thing a serious cook learns here is to keep the chemistry and drop the costume.

The receipts, claim by claim
  • World's best, five times: elBulli topped The World's 50 Best Restaurants in 2002, 2006, 2007, 2008 and 2009 — not "for a decade," five specific years.
  • Closed at the top: shut 30 July 2011, three stars intact, and reopened only as the elBulli Foundation.
  • elBulli1846 is a museum, not a school: the Cala Montjoi site is now "the museum of the restaurant that changed everything." You buy a ticket to visit; nobody cooks or studies there.
  • Spherification wasn't invented at elBulli: the alginate-and-calcium chemistry was documented by Unilever in the 1950s. elBulli brought it into cuisine — it didn't discover it.
  • The book: Modernist Cuisine (Myhrvold, Young & Bilet, 2011) — 6 volumes, 2,438 pages, self-published; James Beard Cookbook of the Year 2012.
Where it went

The restaurant is gone. The technique is everywhere.

elBulli closed, but its cooks scattered and conquered. El Celler de Can Roca, the Roca brothers' three-star in Girona, was named the world's best in 2013 and again in 2015. Disfrutar in Barcelona — opened by three chefs who met working at elBulli — was named the world's best restaurant in 2024. The centre of gravity never left Catalonia.

So the map points where the living scene is: Barcelona, where the diaspora still cooks, teaches and experiments — and where you can actually book a bench and learn the technique, rather than buy a museum ticket to look at where it used to happen.

The honest read

What's technique, and what's theatre

This craft splits cleanly down the middle — and the split was drawn by the founders themselves, which is the strongest honest framing there is.

✓ The technique that lasted

Sous-vide is now standard in serious kitchens; precise low-temperature cooking is simply how you cook a protein well.

Spherification, foams and hydrocolloids are real tools with real chemistry — agar, alginate, lecithin, xanthan — that genuinely entered the fine-dining vocabulary and stayed.

✗ The theatre that dated

"Molecular gastronomy" — a phrase its supposed pioneers disowned in a national newspaper, saying it "does not describe any style of cooking."

Spectacle for its own sake — spherify-everything, dry-ice fog, foam on foam. The 2006 statement warned specifically that liquid nitrogen "should not be used for the sake of novelty."

So the tell is simple: when a diner rolls their eyes at "molecular gastronomy," they're mocking a marketing word the actual masters rejected — not the sous-vide bath that cooked their dinner perfectly. One day versus the other is the whole craft.

The techniques, in full
  • Spherification: a liquid with sodium alginate dropped into a calcium bath; calcium cross-links the alginate into a thin gel skin. "Basic" must be eaten at once; "reverse" (for calcium-rich or acidic liquids) is stable for hours. Chemistry documented by Unilever in the 1950s.
  • Foams / espumas: a flavoured liquid plus a surfactant (lecithin, protein) aerated in a nitrous-oxide siphon — foam without cream or egg. Developed at elBulli in the 1990s.
  • Sous-vide: vacuum-sealed food in a precisely controlled water bath — invented in 1974 by Georges Pralus at Troisgros to stop foie gras losing fat, and refined by scientist Bruno Goussault. Its roots are in industrial food preservation, not art.
  • Hydrocolloids: agar, xanthan, methylcellulose and kin — they raise viscosity or induce gelation, the backbone of modernist texture.
  • Sources: en.wikipedia.org — ElBulli · Ferran Adrià · Modernist Cuisine · Molecular gastronomy · Spherification · Sous vide · the 2006 Statement on the New Cookery (The Observer).
Where we'd send you

Learn the technique, in the city that kept it

★ Our pick for this craft

Vakuum by Martin Lippo

Barcelona · Spain · open courses, private masterclasses & online modules

A working modernist-technique lab run by chef Martin Lippo, in the heart of the living scene. The curriculum is the real thing, module by module — vacuum & sous-vide, foams and carbonation, spherification (basic and advanced), liquid nitrogen, textures — and enrolment is genuinely open: buy an online module outright, or book an in-person class. Not a gatekept stage, not a museum ticket — a place you can actually walk into and cook.

On languageThe online courses run in English; the in-person Barcelona classes are taught in Spanish (English or French only as a private booking). The Basque Culinary Center's "Molecular Cooking" course also runs online in English — so an English speaker has a real route in without leaving the technique behind.

In your futureFine dining now assumes this vocabulary. A cook who genuinely understands spherification, precise temperature and hydrocolloids — not as tricks but as tools — carries a portable, in-demand skill into any serious kitchen.

The courses, and what they cost
  • Online modules (in English): Foams, Sous-Vide, Spherification (basic & advanced), Liquid Nitrogen, Textures — individual modules roughly €85–95 plus theory, or discounted full packs (€210–€552). Bought directly, by anyone.
  • In-person, Barcelona: booked from the catalogue or by arranging a private topic; prices on enquiry; Spanish (English/French privately).

Also real, if not the pick: the Basque Culinary Center in San Sebastián — an institutional heavyweight with avant-garde and "Molecular Cooking" courses (some in English) and open-enrolment short courses. The Upper Room / Sous Vide Australia in Melbourne teaches in English, but leans sous-vide rather than the full modernist range. Not schools, honestly: elBulli1846 is a museum you visit, not a place you study; and El Celler de Can Roca's "stage" is a selective, gatekept placement — they pick you from a CV and a photo — not a course you can book.
