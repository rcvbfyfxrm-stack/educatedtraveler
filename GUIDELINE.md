### (b) Framing: make any skill read as embodied + with-people + at-the-source

The default way the world writes about learning is the trap. "A 5-day pottery course in Tuscany" reads as a product on a shelf — a *course* (a screen could give you that) or a *trip* (a tan that fades). Our framing has to make the reader feel the clay, the room, and the master's hands before they feel a calendar or a cart.

**The three moves that do it, every time:**

1. **Embodied** — lead with the body and the raw material. Hands, heat, salt, grain, rope, stone. Never lead with a curriculum or a syllabus.
2. **With-people** — name the room. A circle, a cohort, the bar, the workshop, the locals already there. Never frame it as you-alone-improving.
3. **At-the-source** — anchor it to the one place on earth where this is most alive, and say *why* it's alive there. Living capital, not a backdrop.

**Before → After rewrites (use these as your ear):**

| Before (product / screen) | After (embodied · with-people · at-the-source) |
|---|---|
| "Learn to make sushi in a 7-day Tokyo course." | "In a Tsukiji back-kitchen, a master watches your knife until the cut is right — beside others chasing the same edge. You don't learn the rice; you learn the room." |
| "Sailing holiday in Croatia — beginners welcome." | "The Adriatic taught Europe to sail. You'll be on the helm by Wednesday, the same channel the old crews ran, in a crew of strangers who'll be your people by Friday." |
| "Pottery retreat in Tuscany. Book your spot." | "The wheel doesn't lie. A week in a Tuscan workshop where the glaze recipes are older than the country — your hands, a teacher's hands over yours, a table of people learning the same patience." |
| "Wellness vacation in Kerala." | "Kerala kept this practice alive for two thousand years. You go where the lineage still breathes, you learn it on your own breath, alongside people who came for the same reason." |
| "Online breadmaking masterclass — lifetime access." | "You can't smell proof through a screen. In a Basque bakery at 4 a.m., the baker shows you the dough at the exact second it's ready — a thing no video can teach." |

Notice the pattern: **a verb your body can feel + a place that earned it + a room you're not alone in + a moment of competence at the end.** That last beat — "by Wednesday," "until the cut is right" — is the payoff. It's also our honest promise: *skills last, tans fade.*

**Words that quietly break the frame** (avoid even when not formally banned): *course, class, session, package, itinerary, getaway, programme.* They reattach the shelf. **Words that hold it:** *room, table, bench, helm, wheel, master, circle, source, by [day] you'll be able to…*

And obey the bans without exception: never *transformation/transformational, life-changing, vacation, holiday, luxury, easy*. On any public surface, never *book, enroll, checkout, price, Quest, XP/level/badge*. We **introduce, connect, bridge** — we never sell, book, or enroll.

### (c) The "worthy of their time & curiosity" bar — and how to open on the payoff

A stranger gives us one second. We earn the second one by being worth it. The bar is simple: **does this teach, surprise, or genuinely move someone toward going — or is it just noise asking to be liked?**

**Earns the click (ship it):**
- A real fact they can carry to a dinner table tonight (the Gilda pintxo named after a Rita Hayworth film — true, cited, and it *means* something).
- A hands moment so tactile it's almost rude not to watch (the Daily Drop's most tactile seconds, ending on the finished object).
- A "wait, *that's* where that's from?" — the source revealed (the one town, the one lineage, the one reason it's alive there).
- A person's honest "I came back able to…" — proof, not promise.

**Noise (kill it):**
- Generic wanderlust ("paradise found"), aspiration with no skill inside it.
- A listicle of "10 places to learn X" with no verified room behind it (this is the skillcation swamp — our biggest drift risk).
- Anything that ends on a screen, a discount, or a vibe instead of a competence.
- A fact we can't verify, dressed as fact (breaks the Honesty Lock — the moat).

**Open on the payoff — always.** The first sentence delivers the reward; it does not warm up to it. Our own live essay is the model: *"Here's something you can carry to your next dinner table."* Then the Gilda. Then the lesson. Never bury the gift under a throat-clear.

- **Bad open:** "San Sebastián is a beautiful coastal city with a rich culinary tradition…"
- **Right open:** "The first pintxo ever named was named after a Rita Hayworth film. Here's why that matters for anyone learning anything well."

If you can't write a first line that gives something away, the piece isn't ready.

### (d) The four content pillars — what each is FOR, and how it feeds the Circle

Four pillars, one job each. Each one ends by handing the reader to the Circle. (The **Daily Drop** is the locked top-of-funnel engine that *carries* these — do not pivot it pre-day-90.)

**1. The Atlas — through the meaning lens.**
*For:* proving the source is real. The Atlas isn't a directory; it's an argument that this craft is *alive here, with these people, and you can go.* We write each entry through the meaning lens — not "what's here" but "why this place earned this craft, and where your cohort gathers."
*Feeds the Circle:* every Atlas page is a destination for the Daily Drop's one URL; the meaning hook ("Living Capital," "Strongest Scenes") is what makes a viewer want more than one place — and the only honest way to get "more" is to join the Circle.
*Stop-doing reminder:* the Atlas is **done** at 99 disciplines / 424 pages. Distribution is progress, not page count. We deepen meaning and verify; we don't add bulk.

**2. Person-led "I came back able to…" stories.**
*For:* turning promise into proof. One person, one place, one real skill they now have. First-person, plain, specific — what their hands could do on day one vs. day seven, who was in the room with them.
*Feeds the Circle:* this is the highest-trust asset we have and the most direct driver of the one metric. It answers "is this real?" with a face. End every story on the Circle, not a hard ask — *"This is the kind of room we map. The Circle is where the next one gets found."*
*Honesty caveat:* never fabricate or composite a person. No verified story yet → don't manufacture one; lean on Atlas + Journal until a real one (or a travelled clip) exists. This is a now-gather, not a now-invent.

**3. The Journal — science-and-stories essays.**
*For:* depth and authority. The reason a craft is alive where it's alive — history, science, lineage, the quiet lesson underneath. Warm, cited, JSON-LD, 4-minute read. San Sebastián is the template: a fact you can carry, then the meaning, then *the same lesson about learning anything well.*
*Feeds the Circle:* the Journal earns the *trust* half of "look, trust, want to go." It's our SEO and our credibility, and it converts the slow reader — the one who needs to know we're serious — into a subscriber. Every essay closes by pointing to the Atlas page it lives near and the Circle.
*Bar:* if there's no verifiable fact and no real lesson, it's a blog post, not a Journal essay. Cut it.

**4. Put-the-screen-down pieces.**
*For:* the quiet foil. Short, sharp, honest reminders that some things can't be learned through glass — *"you can't learn a roll-tack from a video."* This is our differentiation from MasterClass/YouTube, made by feel, not by argument.
*Feeds the Circle:* it reframes the reader's restlessness with screens into a reason to go *do* — and the Circle is the door to doing.
*Stop-doing reminder:* "embodied mastery vs. the AI age" is a **quiet one-line foil, never the billboard.** The market has buzzword fatigue. Drop it in; never build a piece around it as a manifesto.

**The shared rule across all four:** every pillar ends pointing somewhere — Atlas page, Circle, or both. A piece that doesn't hand the reader forward isn't finished. That hand-forward is the only conversion mechanism we have, and it converts into the one metric.

### (e) The curiosity engine — open a loop honestly, never clickbait

Curiosity is the engine; honesty is the brake that keeps it ours. An honest loop **promises something true and then delivers it.** Clickbait promises and withholds, or promises what isn't there. We open loops we can actually close.

**How to open a loop honestly:**
- **Name a real gap the reader feels:** "You can watch a thousand pottery videos and still not know when the clay is ready. There's a reason." → then *give* the reason or the place that teaches it.
- **State a true surprise, then pay it off:** "The world's greatest eating city is built on standing up. Here's what that taught me about learning." (We pay it off — that's our live essay.)
- **Use the Daily Drop's wordless tension honestly:** the clip *is* the loop — hands working raw material — and it closes on the finished object. The competence is the payoff, on screen. No bait; the answer is in the video.

**The honest-loop test (must pass all three):**
1. Is the promise *true*? (Honesty Lock — every fact hand-verified.)
2. Do we *deliver* the payoff in this same piece, or hand it to a real next step (Atlas/Circle)?
3. Would the reader feel *rewarded*, not tricked, after reading?

**Banned curiosity moves:** "You won't believe…", manufactured outrage, fake scarcity, "the one weird trick," withholding a payoff that doesn't exist, any TAM/market-size number (the $75B–$432B figures were refuted — never cite them). One betrayed loop costs us the trust that *is* the business.

> **A note on numbers, everywhere we make things.** Any performance figure you find in 2026 marketing or platform write-ups (engagement multipliers, reach percentages, "best mix" ratios) is from third-party analyses — directional, not gospel. Verify it in the Studio Metrics tab against our own opens and clicks before we ever bet a format on it. Our moat is honesty; never restate someone's blog stat as a settled fact in our copy.

### (f) The focus filter — as an editorial checklist

Run this on every immersion, partner, article, post, clip, Atlas note, and feature *before* it ships. **Any NO = it's out**, regardless of how quick the money or the reach.

- [ ] **Embodied?** — hands and real material are in it (not a concept, not a screen).
- [ ] **Immersive?** — it points to being fully present (a week+, a real room), not an afternoon or a feed.
- [ ] **Community-based?** — there are *peers* in the frame, not a solo improver.
- [ ] **Real-sense transformational?** — the reader/learner comes back able to *do a real thing*. (Internal label only, in our canon Focus Filter vocabulary. We feel it; we never use the word — and this checkbox never gets copy-pasted onto a public surface.)
- [ ] **Master test: does it fight the screen or feed it?** — if it just feeds the scroll, kill it.

**Then the editorial overlay:**

- [ ] **Opens on the payoff?** — first line gives something away.
- [ ] **One idea?** — one piece, one point; everything else cut.
- [ ] **Re-hooks and closes on one thing?** — pulls the reader through and lands on a single takeaway.
- [ ] **Verified?** — every fact hand-checked against the source; anything shaky marked "provisional, verifying," never stated as fact.
- [ ] **Bridge voice?** — introduces/connects/bridges; never sells/books/enrolls; no banned word; customer is the hero, founder is the guide.
- [ ] **Hands forward?** — ends pointing to the Atlas, the Circle, or both.
- [ ] **Moves the one metric?** — a plausible path from this piece to an engaged Circle subscriber. If not, it's noise.

If a piece passes the focus filter but fails "hands forward" or "moves the one metric," it's a nice thing that doesn't serve the mission — fix the ending or don't ship it.

### (b) The content-sharing engine — repost their clips, our frame, clean rights

The daily clip is the engine that feeds the one metric. Schools are our richest source of footage *and* our biggest distribution multiplier when they reshare. Here's how to run it without ever crossing a rights line.

#### Rights & permission hygiene (the 2026 norms, applied)

A repost is not consent and a tag is not a transfer of rights — copyright vests automatically in whoever shot the clip, and using it commercially without an explicit, written, *scoped* grant exposes you to real liability ([Showcase](https://www.showca.se/post/ugc-usage-rights), [Later](https://later.com/blog/user-generated-content-rules/)). So ET's rule is simple and non-negotiable: **no clip is touched before a written yes that names the boundaries.** A defensible license names platforms, duration, whether edits are allowed, and geography ([Digital Applied](https://www.digitalapplied.com/blog/ugc-rights-licensing-framework-2026-brand-creator-guide)). Our reply-thread grant does exactly that, in plain founder English:

> **Footage permission — plain terms (paste into the email reply):**
> By replying "yes," you give EducatedTraveler a non-exclusive license to edit and post the clip(s) you send — short films on our social accounts (TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, our site) and our newsletter, worldwide, for as long as we both want. You keep full copyright; you can post the clips anywhere yourself. We always credit and tag you. No paid ads without asking you first. Any music we add is licensed by us. If anyone recognisable is in frame, please confirm they're fine being shown.

That single paragraph covers scope, duration, modification rights, geography, non-exclusivity, paid-ad carve-out, music clearance, and likeness clearance — the full 2026 checklist ([Showcase](https://www.showca.se/post/ugc-usage-rights), [The Social Media Law Firm](https://thesocialmedialawfirm.com/blog/social-media-compliance/ugc-legal-risks-and-compliance-a-2026-guide/)). Keep every "yes" thread; the Studio Outreach tracker logs *date, school, scope, link to thread*. Turn the messy DM into a repeatable record — capture request, store consent, publish with attribution ([Automateed](https://www.automateed.com/reposting-rules-for-user-generated-content)).

*(One caveat on all of the above: these are guidance pages and marketing/legal write-ups, not legal authority. The plain-terms grant is a sensible default, not a substitute for a real review — before we lean on it at any scale, have a lawyer look at it. General guidance, not legal advice.)*

**Two modes, two rights levels:**
- **Reposting their public clip as-is** (Instagram's native repost — reported live since around Aug 2025; verify the current feature/date before you rely on it) — light use, still ask first, credit visibly in the caption and tag them in-frame; burying credit in hashtags doesn't count ([Later](https://later.com/blog/user-generated-content-rules/)).
- **Re-editing their footage into an ET film** — this is the licensed path above; needs the written grant because we're modifying and distributing off their platform.

#### Attribution rules (always, every time)

1. **In the caption, first line of credit:** "Footage: @{school}, {place}." Visible, not in hashtags.
2. **Tag the school** in the post and in-frame where the platform allows.
3. **Atlas link** to their page is already live — the caption's one URL points there, sending warm traffic both to them and to the Circle sign-up.
4. **Their cut is theirs:** when we send the finished film, they may repost with no obligation to credit us back. Generosity compounds trust.
5. **If a school ever asks to be removed** — clip down within 24h, no questions. Honesty Lock.

#### The consistent ET frame (so a school's clip still reads as "EducatedTraveler")

Every shared clip — ours or theirs — is wrapped in the same locked Daily Drop format so the feed has one face: 30–60s vertical, **wordless**, hands + raw material, the most tactile seconds, **end on the finished object / moment of competence**, one place-name caption card max, **ambient sound only**. We don't pivot this pre-day-90. The school's footage becomes ET-recognisable purely through this frame + the look below — without ever overwriting their craft with our branding.

- and — the strongest signal — **our clip of them genuinely travelled** (a Phase-2 gate in itself), or their craft drew unprompted "can you connect me?" inbound to the Circle.

None of this is ever communicated as "you're being evaluated as a partner." It's just relationship-building. The shortlist is a private byproduct. When the 8 Phase-2 gates finally turn green together, we already have a ranked, trust-tested list of one or two schools who've shown they're real — and we open rung 6 to them first.

**The discipline that protects all of it:** every rung 1–5 move must be able to stand alone as a gift. The day a school can tell we were only being generous to set up a sale, the moat is gone. Generosity now is what makes the Phase-2 ask land later — and what keeps the Circle filling with people who open, click, and refer between now and then.

## Part 4 — The Community: a social home for skills

Here is the one thing to hold onto before any feature: **we are not building a feed. We are building a room where people who chase the same fire can show each other their hands at work.** The feed is just the wall they pin things to. So the test for every post, prompt, and pixel in here is the master test from the Focus Filter: **does it fight the screen or feed it?** A skills-only social home that turns into another dopamine treadmill has betrayed its own reason to exist.

**The vision, in one line:** *The Circle grows from a list you read into a room you post in — a place, a person, your people, made visible.* Not "social media for everyone about everything," but the opposite: social media for one person at a time chasing one real craft, anchored to one real place on the Atlas.

### (b) The feed model — what a "skill post" is

A skill post is **not a status update**. It is the smallest unit of "I did a real thing with my hands today." Four post types, and only four, so the room never drifts into chatter:

1. **Progress** — a short clip or before→competence image of you actually doing the craft. ("First clean roll-tack. Took me forty tries.")
2. **A hard-won fact** — one concrete thing you learned at the source, the kind no video gives you. ("The miso changes flavour by the hour in summer — the master taps the lid, doesn't open it.")
3. **A beautiful image** — the material, the tool, the place, the finished object. Tactile, not staged.
4. **A real question** — pointed, answerable by a peer or a master. ("Why does my knife drag on the pull and not the push?")

**Every post must anchor to the Atlas.** This is the load-bearing rule and our entire moat against the skillcation-listicle swamp. A post cannot be published without selecting:
- a **discipline** (one of 99), which sets the **core** colour (wellness #94ad86 / adventure #6fa3a0 / creative #cf8f6e / culinary #c9a24a), and
- a **place** (an Atlas destination — the source, a school, or "where I am working it").

That single requirement does three things at once: it ties the social home to the real map instead of the void, it routes the post into the right per-craft room, and it makes every post a quiet ambassador for an Atlas page (which serves the one metric — engaged subs find real masters).

**The structure — per-craft, rolling up to per-core:**

```
CORE: Culinary (#c9a24a)
  └─ Discipline room: Sushi — Tokyo / Kanazawa
        └─ skill posts, chronological, anchored to Atlas place
  └─ Discipline room: Bread — San Sebastián
CORE: Adventure (#6fa3a0)
  └─ Discipline room: Big-boat sailing — Mediterranean
```

A member lives mostly in their **discipline room** (their people). The **core view** is the wider neighbourhood they can wander into. There is no "everything" view — that absence is a feature, not a gap.

**Surfacing — by community-rank, never popularity** (Honesty Lock). The Pinboard orders posts by:
- **Recency within your discipline room** first (you see your people, now).
- **Community-rank of the place** second — the same five-dot "best chance to find your cohort" signal the Atlas already uses, NOT a like-count or reputation score.
- **"A peer replied" boost** third — a post that drew a real answer rises, because a conversation is the thing we want more of.

No engagement score. No "trending." No view counts shown. The question we ask of the ordering is the same as everywhere: does this pull someone toward *doing*, or toward scrolling?

### (c) Mechanics that keep it authentic, not generic social

The 2026 evidence is blunt: infinite scroll and algorithmic feeds work like slot machines — intermittent, uncertain reward firing the dopamine loop — and the EU has signalled it intends to act on dark patterns and addictive design (a "Digital Fairness Act" has been floated alongside a fitness-check on existing rules; treat the exact name, status and effect as provisional and verify before quoting it) ([Jumpstart](https://www.jumpstartmag.com/hooked-on-the-scroll-inside-the-dopamine-loop), [The Week](https://theweek.com/culture-life/personal-technology/end-infinite-scroll-doomscrolling)). We build to that standard now, by choice, not because a regulator made us. Calm-technology principle: the room should sit at the periphery and reward leaving it, not trap attention at the centre ([Jumpstart](https://www.jumpstartmag.com/hooked-on-the-scroll-inside-the-dopamine-loop)).

Concrete mechanics:

- **Finite, not infinite.** The Pinboard loads one cohort's posts, then *ends*. A clear "you're caught up — go make something" card at the bottom. No auto-load, no "load more" pulling you down a hole.
- **Quality gate over reach.** Posts that aren't doing-the-craft (selfies, links, hot takes, "good morning") simply have no post type to live in — the four-type structure refuses them by design.
- **No follower counts, no like tallies, ever.** A post can be marked **"this helped me"** by a peer — but the count is private to the author, never a public number. Recognition, not a scoreboard.
- **Real-name, real-progress culture.** Profiles carry real names and a real craft. The crew card's "what I'm working on right now" line is the social object — progress, not persona.
- **Prompts that pull toward DOING.** The weekly ritual is a *task*, not a topic: "Post your worst attempt this week." "One fact your master gave you that a video never would." "Show the tool, not the trophy." This is straight from what works in small communities — rituals that lower the effort to participate and ask for *specific* contributions, not generic intros ([innoloft](https://innoloft.com/en-us/blog/how-to-build-community), [mean.ceo](https://blog.mean.ceo/building-customer-community-guide/)).
- **No notifications-as-bait.** One digest, not a stream of pings. A weekly "in your room this week" email (rides the existing Circle/Resend sender) — which doubles as direct fuel for the one metric: opens and clicks.
- **Slowness is the brand.** Daily Drop stays the wordless tactile clip; the feed inherits its DNA — hands, material, the moment of competence — never talking-heads or text walls.

> The line to hold: **quality over reach, the source over the screen, your people over an audience.** Everything cold, glassy, gamified, or Netflix-shaped is retired forever.

### (d) Moderation, safety, trust at solo-founder scale

Arnaud is solo, part-time, often at sea. Moderation must be lightweight, honest, and defensible — and the literature is clear that trust infrastructure has to exist *before* the first incident, not be bolted on after ([mean.ceo](https://blog.mean.ceo/niche-community-building-fans/)). The good news: a small, anchored, real-name room is most of the safety by construction.

**Structural safety (does the work without the founder):**
- **Members only.** Posting requires a Supabase account in a cohort — no anonymous drive-by, which kills most abuse before it starts.
- **Real name + real craft + Atlas anchor.** Three friction points that filter out spam and bad actors without a moderation team.
- **Small rooms.** Per-discipline scale means peers self-correct; the founder is a presence, not a police force.

**Lightweight human layer:**
- **One page of house rules, in the founder voice.** Short: this is a room for people doing real things; be kind, be specific, anchor to a real place; no selling, no spam, no cruelty. Visible at the door (set the tone early — clear rules, visible moderation, respectful-communication guidance ([bettermode](https://bettermode.com/blog/grow-successful-online-community))).
- **Report → quiet queue.** A single "flag" on any post drops it into a Studio queue (build on the existing studio.html cockpit). Flagged posts auto-soften (hidden from the room, visible to author) pending a founder glance — fail-safe, not fail-open.
- **Rate limits, not algorithms.** A few posts per member per day. Stops flooding without judging content.
- **Honest absence.** If the founder is at sea for a week, the room is told: "Arnaud's offshore — slower replies, same room." Honesty is the moat; we never fake presence or counts.

**Trust mechanics tied to the Honesty Lock:**
- Self-declared skills/credentials stay clearly *self-declared* (current free-text reality).
- Only the **instructor-VERIFIED flag** on prior-experiences carries weight, and it's marked as such — never inflated into a fake badge.
- No fabricated member counts, no "10k+ makers" puffery. We show what's real.

### (e) Seeding, cold-start, and the bridges out

Cold-start is where most communities die quietly — and the fix is well-documented: seed before you launch, invite a narrow first cohort, ask for specific contributions, and expect 6–12 months to real life ([innoloft](https://innoloft.com/en-us/blog/how-to-build-community), [mean.ceo](https://blog.mean.ceo/building-customer-community-guide/)). Apply it literally:

**Seeding (now-move):**
1. **Founder goes first, daily, for weeks.** Arnaud posts real progress from his own crafts (cooking, sailing) anchored to real Atlas places. The room must be *full of doing* before anyone is invited in — empty rooms broadcast death.
2. **Narrow first cohort.** Pull the most engaged existing Circle subs (the openers/clickers — they're already identified in Resend/analytics). Invite by hand, by name, into one discipline room. Not a broadcast.
3. **Specific asks, not "introduce yourself."** First prompt: "Post one thing you made this month and the one place you'd go to get better at it." This produces Atlas-anchored posts on day one.
4. **Instructor cross-post (now-move, no money).** Use the existing invite-instructor edge function. A verified school/master shares a single learning clip or fact into the relevant discipline room. This is the spine made real — *a person* shows up where *your people* are — and it warms the room with authority, not ads.

**Bridge: feed → IRL** (phase-gated later-move). A "who's-going / local circles" mechanism on the discipline room: when a place lights up with posts, members can quietly raise a hand for an in-person gathering at the source. This is the IRL connection mechanism that's a build target — and it only matters once rooms are alive (Phase 3 / approaching the 8-gate).

**Bridge: feed → Skill Passport.** A **progress** post can be promoted by its author into their **prior-experiences** record. If a master/instructor saw it, the instructor-VERIFIED flag can attach. The feed becomes the *evidence stream* that feeds the credential — the CREDENTIAL + CURATION + COHORT fusion no incumbent stacks. Honest, earned, not bought.

> Both bridges feed the one metric in opposite directions: IRL gives the "can you connect me?" inbound the Phase-2 gate watches for; the Passport gives subscribers a reason to stay engaged for months, not days.

## Part 5 — The Skill Passport: profile + credentials

A crew card is a snapshot. A passport is a record. The move here is to take the read-only crew card you already ship at `/crew` and turn it into one public page a person is proud to put in their bio, in their email signature, on their CV — a page that says *here is what I can actually do, here is who taught me, and here is the proof.* Skills last, tans fade. The passport is where that line stops being a slogan and becomes a document.

One discipline before anything else: **the passport's only job is to make the honesty visible.** Every other publication on earth lets you type "expert" into a box. We don't. The instant a stranger can't trust a line on the page, the page is worthless — and so is the moat. So we build the profile first, and we build the credential model second and slower, and we refuse to issue a single thing we can't stand behind. That refusal *is* the product.

### (a) The public profile — link-in-bio that earns the next click

Today `/crew` is a read-only card the visitor lands on cold. The passport is the same data, reframed as a **landing page a holder actively shares** — a Linktree that happens to be a verifiable record.

**Share URL.** Keep it human and ownable. The holder picks a handle at the profile; the page lives at:

A vanity slug (not the Supabase UUID) is the single thing that makes a page *shareable* — nobody puts `?id=8f3a-...` in their bio. Add a slug column to `profiles`, unique, claimed once. The QR posters you already cut (`marketing/qr/qr-crew-*`) point here. The existing `/crew?id=` route 301s to `/p/<slug>` so nothing breaks.

**What the page shows, top to bottom** (one idea per block — never lose focus):

**The funnel — every link points home.** A Linktree usually scatters people *outward* to a dozen platforms. That is the whole game: **the passport turns one member into a recruiting surface for the next.** Word-of-mouth is the cheapest channel a solo founder has, and the passport is the artifact that carries it.

- **Atlas** → each skill links to its Atlas discipline page; each `prior_experiences` location links to its Atlas destination. The passport *feeds the Atlas its inbound links* and the Atlas gives the passport its credibility ("this place is real, here it is on the map").

### (b) The credential model — four tiers, brutally honest

The current state is the right foundation: `profiles.credentials` is free text, `profiles.existing_certs` is free text, and only `prior_experiences.verified` carries real weight (an instructor flips it on a row they can vouch for). We keep that exact mechanic and wrap a **tier ladder** around it. The tier is not decoration — it changes how the claim is *displayed and described* to a stranger.

| Tier | Name | Source of truth | How it shows | What it honestly means |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0** | **Self-declared** | The holder typed it (`skills[]`, `credentials` text) | Plain chip, label *"Self-declared"* | "This person says they can do this." Nothing more. |
| **1** | **Community-vouched** | Peers in the holder's cohort confirm it | Chip + small count, *"Vouched by 3 in the circle"* | Social signal, not proof. Honest *because* we label it as soft. |
| **2** | **Instructor-verified** | The teaching master / school flips the existing `verified` flag | Chip + **✓ Verified by [Instructor], [School]** + the Atlas link | "The person who taught them confirms they were there and did the work." This is the strong, real tier. |
| **3** | **ET-issued** | EducatedTraveler signs a credential | ✓✓ **EducatedTraveler · Completion of [curated immersion]** | The *only* thing ET issues — and only for what ET can legitimately attest. **Phase-2-gated.** |

**The honesty rules — read these twice, because breaking one breaks the moat:**

1. **ET can certify ATTENDANCE and PARTICIPATION at things ET ran. ET cannot certify MASTERY.** A Tier 3 record would read, hypothetically, *"completed the ET [craft] immersion in [place], [dates], with [master]"* — a record of presence and engagement at a curated ET immersion. (No such immersion exists or is scheduled today; this is an illustrative shape only — Tier 3 is hard-gated, see the phasing below.) It does **not** say *"certified sushi chef."* We did not assess a skill against a standard; we hosted a week. Say exactly that and no more.

2. **The teacher owns mastery, not us.** If anyone gets to attest "this person can actually do the thing," it is the master who watched them do it — Tier 2, the instructor flag. ET's job is curation and connection, not examination. This maps to the founder's real moat (media/curation) and stays inside the strategy LOCK: *introduce / connect / bridge,* never assess-and-certify like a credentialing body we are not.

3. **Never let a lower tier wear a higher tier's clothes.** A self-declared "expert woodworker" must look visibly different from an instructor-verified one. The tier glyph and label are mandatory and non-removable. The day a Tier 0 chip is indistinguishable from a Tier 2 chip, we are a résumé-inflation site, and we have become exactly the *"certificate from an unknown online platform with no industry recognition"* that — per 2026 third-party analyses (directional, verify in Studio Metrics) — employers report discounting ([Sertifier, 2026](https://sertifier.com/blog/credentials-2026-digital-trust-verification/)).

4. **No issuance for watching.** One 2026 hiring write-up argues employers do not value *"credentials that require nothing more than watching videos and paying a fee"* ([The Interview Guys, 2026](https://blog.theinterviewguys.com/micro-credentials-that-actually-get-you-hired/)) — treat that as a directional claim to validate, not a settled law, but it points the right way. Tier 3 is gated behind a real, embodied, week+, at-the-source immersion that passes the FOCUS FILTER. If it doesn't return a real skill — embodied, immersive, community-based — it cannot earn an ET credential. The Focus Filter is the credential's admissions test.

5. **Revocation is a feature, not an embarrassment.** A teacher can un-verify. ET can revoke a Tier 3. A verifiable credential the holder can't lose is just marketing; one that can be revoked is trustworthy. We build revocation in from day one (see standards below).

**The credential-inflation trap, named so we never walk into it:** the temptation, the moment a few hundred people hold passports, will be to mint "EducatedTraveler Certified ___" because it looks impressive and people will ask for it. Resist completely. The instant ET attests to a skill it never assessed, the verified mark means nothing, and the one thing we have that VAWAA, MasterClass and the skillcation-listicle swamp don't — *trustable proof* — evaporates. **We would rather issue ten credentials a year that are bulletproof than a thousand that a hiring manager rolls their eyes at.**

### (c) The standards — portable, provable, low-ops

Three things appear true in 2026 and worth building on (each is provider/standards-body documentation — confirm the exact status and dates at build time, since these move):

- **Open Badges 3.0** was reported voted Final by 1EdTech in mid-2024 and is now widely treated as the dominant skill-credential standard, with cryptographic signing built in ([1EdTech impl guide](https://www.imsglobal.org/spec/ob/v3p0/impl); [virtualbadge.io, 2026](https://www.virtualbadge.io/blog-articles/open-badges-3-0-what-is-the-status-in-2026)). Verify the "Final" status with 1EdTech before relying on it.
- **Open Badges 3.0 is itself a W3C Verifiable Credential.** It is designed to align with the W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model v2.0, which is reported to have reached W3C Recommendation in mid-May 2025 — same `issuer` / `credentialSubject` / `achievement` / `proof` / `credentialStatus` shape ([Sertifier](https://sertifier.com/blog/open-badges-3-explained/); [VC Data Model status](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-bitstring-status-list/)). Confirm the exact Recommendation date against W3C at build time. If it holds, adopting OB 3.0 gets us VC compliance for free — one standard, two boxes ticked.
- **Trust comes from issuer identity, evidence, and stable identifiers — not from a badge image.** The 2026 field guidance is explicit that a PDF or a pretty PNG is worthless; verifiers check *who signed it, what the evidence was, and whether the identifier resolves* ([Sertifier credentials guide, 2026](https://sertifier.com/blog/credentials-2026-digital-trust-verification/)).

**What that means for a Tier-3 ET credential (Phase 2):** issue it as an **Open Badges 3.0 credential**, which gives us, in one JSON object:

- `issuer` → ET's identity, anchored with **`did:web`** keyed to `educatedtraveler.app`. This is the low-ops choice: a `did:web` is just a signed JSON file served from your own domain — no blockchain, no fees, no third party. The conformance guidance even recommends `did:web` on the issuer's own web domain precisely *to avoid portability challenges* ([UN Transparency Protocol VC spec](https://untp.unece.org/docs/0.6.0/specification/VerifiableCredentials/)).
- `credentialSubject.achievement` → the honest claim ("Completed the EducatedTraveler [craft] immersion, [place], [dates], with [master/school]").
- **`evidence`** → an `evidence` URL pointing back to the holder's passport / the Atlas page / the Daily-Drop clip of their work. Evidence is what separates a real credential from a participation sticker.
- **`proof`** → ET's cryptographic signature. Sign once at issuance with the `did:web` key.
- **`credentialStatus`** → a **Bitstring Status List** (the W3C revocation mechanism) — a single small file at `educatedtraveler.app/credentials/status/1` that lists which credentials are still valid. To revoke one, flip one bit. It's privacy-preserving and storage-agnostic; the issuer just serves it from a URL ([W3C Bitstring Status List](https://www.w3.org/TR/vc-bitstring-status-list/); [walt.id status docs](https://docs.walt.id/concepts/credential-lifecycle/credential-status-and-revocation)).

**The concrete low-ops path for a solo founder (do NOT build a credentialing platform):**

1. **Don't self-build crypto.** For the handful of Tier-3 credentials Phase 2 will produce, **use a managed OB 3.0 issuer** (Accredible, Canvas Credentials/Badgr, Credly, POK — reported as certified 3.0 issuers in 2026; confirm certification before choosing) OR a small open-source signer (`walt.id`, `transmute status-list.vc`). A solo founder issuing dozens, not millions, of credentials should not run key infrastructure by hand.
2. **Anchor issuer identity as `did:web`** under your own domain — one static signed file, version-controlled in the repo, deployed via the existing gh-clone-to-/private/tmp recipe. No new infra.
3. **Serve the status list as a static file** on the same GitHub Pages site. Revocation = edit one file, redeploy. This fits your stack exactly — it's the same deploy muscle as the Atlas.
4. **LinkedIn "Add to profile."** This is the high-leverage integration and it costs nothing: LinkedIn's *Add to Profile* lets an issuer generate a pre-filled link (name, org, issue date, credential ID, credential URL) that drops the credential straight into a member's *Licenses & Certifications* section ([addtoprofile.linkedin.com](https://addtoprofile.linkedin.com/); [LinkedIn Help](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a567169)). Generate one button per Tier-3 credential. As of the latest provider guidance, the public Add-to-Profile link reportedly needs no API approval (the full Profile Edit API is gated to approved partners, which we don't need) — **verify this against LinkedIn's current terms at build time, since their policy changes.** The credential URL points back to the holder's ET passport, so LinkedIn becomes a *funnel back to us.*

**Tier 0–2 need none of this.** They are not signed credentials — they are honest, labelled claims living in your Supabase rows, rendered on the passport. Don't over-engineer them. Open Badges machinery is reserved for Tier 3, where a stranger needs cryptographic proof an institution stands behind it.

**For the holder (work / CV):**
- The passport URL goes in an email signature, a CV, a portfolio, an Instagram bio. It says *"I spent a real week learning a real thing from a real master"* — which, per 2026 third-party trend analyses (directional, verify in Studio Metrics), is the kind of non-degree signal a skills-based hiring market is said to weight more heavily ([virtualbadge.io trends, 2026](https://www.virtualbadge.io/blog-articles/top-5-digital-credentialing-trends-in-2026-ai-micro-credentials-and-more)).

- A Tier-3 OB 3.0 credential pushes to LinkedIn in two clicks and travels anywhere — it's a portable W3C VC, not a walled-garden badge.

**For the verifier (a hiring manager, a school, a charter captain checking a chef):**
- Tier 0–2: they open the passport, see the tier labels, and click through. An instructor-verified row links to the named instructor and the Atlas school card — they can contact the source. **Transparency is the verification.**
- Tier 3: they paste the credential into any W3C VC verifier (or click "verify" on the passport), which resolves ET's `did:web`, checks the signature, and checks the Bitstring Status List for revocation. Green = ET signed this, it hasn't been pulled, here's the evidence URL. This is the *"who signed it, what's the evidence, does the identifier resolve"* test the 2026 guidance demands — and we pass it by construction.

Earned, not bought — including the right to issue the credential itself.

## Part 6 — IRL: connecting like-minded people

### The whole point was never the screen

Everything we build online — the Atlas, the Daily Drop, the Circle — is a means, not the end. The end is two people who were chasing the same fire finding each other, then standing in the same room with their hands in the same clay. "A place, a person, your people." Parts 1 through 5 earned the attention. This Part spends it: it turns a click into a coffee, a coffee into a practice circle, and — once the 8-gate opens — a practice circle into a week at the source.

One rule governs every rung below: **IRL is not a new product. It is the deepest expression of the one metric.** A member who shows up in person, meets a peer, and comes home glad will open the next email, click the next Atlas page, and tell one friend. So every rung here is judged the same way as a post or an article — does it produce **engaged Circle subscribers**, and does it pass the **focus filter** (embodied, immersive, community-based, returns a real skill)? If a rung feeds the screen instead of fighting it, it's out.

And the honest constraint that shapes all of it: Arnaud is solo, part-time, often at sea. **The founder cannot be in the room.** So the entire IRL design is built to run *without* him — light enough that a volunteer host carries it, safe enough that strangers can meet without ET becoming an operator, and instrumented so the proof flows back into the feed on its own.

> **On the comparison figures below:** every external number in this Part (MEA, AI Tinkerers, meetup programs) comes from third-party 2026 pages and is directional, not gospel — a pattern to learn from, not a metric to bet the format on. Verify any figure in the Studio Metrics tab before you lean on it. Our own numbers are the only ones we treat as fact, and our honesty lock applies here as much as it does to Atlas facts.

### (a) The offline ladder — lightest to deepest

Five rungs. Each is a smaller ask than the next, and each one a member climbs is a stronger Circle subscriber. The first four are **now-moves** (they need no money, no booking, no operations). The fifth is **Phase-2-gated** and stays dark on the public site until all 8 gates are green.

| Rung | What it is | The ask | Founder ops | Gate |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Near me | "Who else near me is into X" — a soft signal, never a directory | Tick a box | Zero (passive) | NOW |
| 2. Who's going | A "who's going / who's been" marker on Atlas places | Tap a heart | Zero (passive) | NOW |
| 3. Practice circle | Informal local skill meetup — members run it themselves | Show up | Near-zero (template only) | NOW (soft, opt-in seed) |
| 4. ET gathering | A small ET-hosted in-person evening | Show up | Light (1 event, founder-presence optional) | NOW (occasional, when capacity allows) |
| 5. The immersion | The cohort week at the source, co-hosted with a vetted partner | Travel together | Heavy — Phase-2 only | GATED (8-gate) |

**Rung 1 — "Who else near me is into X."** Inside `community.html`, a member can opt in to a single, coarse signal: *city or metro region* (never precise location) + their top one or two crafts. The product never shows a list of named strangers. It shows a **count and a nudge**: "3 others near Lisbon are chasing wood-fired ceramics. Want us to tell you when there are enough to gather?" That's it. The count is the hook; the connection is consent-gated (see (c)). This rung costs nothing to run and converts passive readers into members who've raised their hand — the exact behaviour the one metric measures. It builds on what's already live: the profile already stores `interests` and `skills[]`, so this is a query and a toggle, not a new system.

**Rung 2 — "Who's going / who's been" on Atlas places.** The Atlas is 324 destinations of cold facts today. We warm one corner of it with a single peer signal. On a destination or school card, a member can mark — privately, opt-in — *"I've been here"* or *"I want to go."* The card then shows an aggregate, never names: "4 from the Circle have trained here · 11 want to go." This is the lightest possible expression of "your people," and it's pure focus-filter fuel — it tells a reader that a real human peer walked through that door. It also feeds the community-RANK honesty (best chance to find your cohort), not a reputation score. Crucially, it does **not** become a booking widget or re-surface selling — it's a heartbeat, not a checkout. (This is the *only* new thing we add to the Atlas; the stop-doing list forbids building more Atlas pages, and we honour it.)

**Rung 3 — Practice circles (local skill meetups).** When a city crosses a small threshold of members chasing the same craft (start the threshold honestly low — 3 to 5), we don't run an event. We *invite a host*. A practice circle is a recurring, member-run gathering: a few people who knead, throw, sail, sketch, or cook *together*, monthly, in a real place a member already has (a studio, a kitchen, a beach, a park). ET supplies the spark, the template, and the introduction — nothing more. This is the load-bearing rung: it's where "community-based" stops being a slogan. The volunteer playbook for it is in (b).

**Rung 4 — ET-hosted small gatherings.** Occasionally — when Arnaud is ashore in a city with a real cluster, or a trusted host steps up — ET puts its name on one evening: a screening of Daily Drops on a wall, a hands-on demo from a local master we've Atlas-verified, a long table. Small (8–20), warm, no selling, no pitch. The point is to let members feel the brand as a *room*, not a feed, and to recruit practice-circle hosts from the people who light up that night. Founder presence is **optional** — a host can run it from the same template. We keep these rare on purpose; rarity protects founder capacity and makes each one matter.

**Rung 5 — The cohort immersion (GATED).** This is the destination of the whole ladder: a week+ at the source, with a vetted partner master, alongside the peers a member already met on rungs 1–4. It is **off-site, referral/co-host fee only, never an on-site transaction**, and it stays invisible on the public site until every one of the 8 gates is true — including gate (4), *regular unprompted "can you connect me?" inbound*, which the lower rungs are designed to manufacture. We do not soften this. Until the gate opens, the answer to "how do I do the immersion?" is "join a practice circle and come to a gathering — that's where it starts." Honest, and it drives the one metric instead of revenue we can't yet serve.

> The ladder's logic: each rung is a smaller, warmer ask that produces a stronger signal of intent. By the time someone has been to three practice circles, the immersion isn't a sale — it's the obvious next step among friends. That's "earned, not bought," made literal.

### (b) Local Circles / chapters — self-organising with minimal founder ops

The model to adapt — honestly — is **MEA's**: per MEA's own pages, dozens of volunteer-run regional chapters (figures vary by source and are approximate) across a reported ~60 countries serving a reported 8,000+ alumni, with chapter events posted on a shared portal calendar and members meeting for dinners, hikes, talks, and structured conversations entirely on their own steam ([MEA Wisdom Community](https://www.meawisdom.com/community/), [MEA alumni portal](https://alumni.modernelderacademy.com/)). The same volunteer-host pattern reportedly runs AI Tinkerers' large multi-city network and the WordPress and FSF meetup programs — events "organized by any reliable, trusted member of the community" off a provided guide and a safe-space policy template ([WordPress Meetup Program Basics](https://make.wordpress.org/community/handbook/meetup-organizer/meetup-program-basics/), [FSF LibreLocal](https://www.fsf.org/blogs/community/librelocal-2026-is-in-may-start-organizing-meetups)). The lesson across all of them — and the part we actually borrow — is structural, not the headcount: **the founder supplies a kit and an introduction; volunteers supply the room.** That is the only way a solo, at-sea founder runs a global IRL community.

But we adapt it honestly. MEA chapters are bound by a shared *workshop experience* members already paid for. We have no shared experience yet — so a Circle chapter is bound by a shared **craft**, not a shared alumni status. A chapter is "Lisbon · Ceramics," not "Lisbon." That keeps the focus filter intact: every chapter exists to practise one real thing together.

**How a chapter is born (seeded by online activity):**
1. Rung-1 signals cross threshold in a city for one craft (e.g. 5 members near Lisbon ticked ceramics).
2. The Studio cockpit surfaces it on a new **"Sparks" view** (Metrics-tab sibling) — a ranked list of city × craft clusters ripe to seed.
3. ET emails the cluster one message: *"There are now enough of you near Lisbon chasing wood-fired ceramics to gather. Would one of you host the first practice circle? Here's everything you need."*
4. First volunteer to say yes becomes the **host**. ET introduces them to the others (consent-gated, see (c)) and hands over the playbook. Done. No founder presence required.

**The volunteer host playbook (one page, lives at `community.html` → "Host a circle"):**
- **What a circle is:** 3–8 people, one craft, one recurring meet (aim monthly), in a real place *you already have access to* — your studio, a kitchen, a beach, a park, a community room. ET does not rent space or pay for anything.
- **Your job (small):** pick a date, pick the place, tell people what to bring, show up, make it warm. That's all. You are a host, not a teacher and not a guide.
- **Your first message template** (ET supplies it pre-written): time, place, what to bring, what we'll make/practise, "all levels welcome, we're here to do the thing together."
- **The four house rules** (printed on the kit, non-negotiable): (1) we make/practise something real every time — no talking-shop-only meets; (2) all levels welcome; (3) the safe-space policy applies (see (e)); (4) no selling, no recruiting, no MLM — this is a craft circle, not a pitch.
- **After each meet:** post one photo or 30-second clip to the chapter thread and tag it #FromTheCircle. (This is the loop-back engine — see (f). It's the *only* thing ET asks back.)
- **What ET gives you:** the intro to your members, the message templates, the safe-space policy, a printable one-pager, a spot on the Circle calendar, and a feature in the newsletter when your circle does something good. **What ET does not give you:** money, insurance, space, or instruction. You're the host; ET is the matchmaker.

**Minimal-ops guarantees.** A chapter must survive Arnaud being at sea for a month. So: hosts self-schedule on a shared calendar; the chapter thread is member-run (reuse the existing cohort-chat realtime infra, scoped to a `chapter_id`); and ET's only recurring obligation is the monthly "Sparks" review in Studio (15 minutes) plus the occasional new-host intro. No approvals, no event-by-event sign-off. If a host goes quiet, the circle either self-revives or sleeps — and a sleeping circle costs nothing.

**Tie to the one metric:** every chapter is a retention machine. A member in an active practice circle is the highest-open, highest-click, highest-referral subscriber we have — and each circle is a standing recruiter (members bring craft-friends, who join the Circle to find the circle). Chapters don't dilute the metric; they compound it.

### (c) The matching / "your people" mechanic — privacy, consent, safety-first

The single biggest way to wreck trust — and trust *is* the moat — is to turn "your people" into surveillance. So the mechanic is built on three hard rules, in order of priority: **opt-in, no exposure without consent, safety-first for strangers meeting.**

**Rule 1 — Opt-in, granular, reversible.** Nothing is shared by default. The profile already has a `visibility` field (private / cohort / public) — we extend it, never weaken it. A member separately opts in to: *(a)* appearing in near-me counts, *(b)* the who's-going Atlas markers, *(c)* being introduced for a practice circle. Each toggle is independent and instantly reversible. Default for all three: **off.**

**Rule 2 — Counts before names, double-opt-in before introductions.** The product shows aggregates ("3 near you," "11 want to go") — never a named list of strangers a member can browse. There is **no member directory, no map of people, no search-for-humans.** An introduction only happens when *both* sides consent in the same moment: ET says "Maria near you wants to start a ceramics circle — share your first name and contact with her?" and the connection is made *only* if both tap yes. This mirrors widely-used meetup-safety norms — minimise public exposure of who's attending and let people verify before they're identifiable ([Meetup safety practices](https://www.meetup.com/topics/safety/us/)). Borrow it exactly.

**Rule 3 — Safety-first for strangers meeting.** Before any first IRL introduction, ET shows a short, plain safety card (not buried in a ToS): meet first in a public place; tell a friend where you're going; you can decline or leave anytime; here's how to report. Hosts get a slightly fuller version. This is non-negotiable and it's cheap — it's a screen of copy, and it signals seriousness, which deepens trust rather than scaring people off.

**What the mechanic must NOT be** (guardrails, stated plainly so we never drift): not a dating feature; not a "people near you" map; not a follower/friend graph; not a leaderboard of who's most active; not anything that surfaces a stranger's identity, location, or schedule without that stranger's explicit yes. If a build idea violates any of these, it's out — same as a focus-filter NO, regardless of engagement it might juice.

This restraint is itself the product. In a 2026 where every community app is fighting accusations of surveillance and creep, **"we show you counts, not people, and never introduce anyone without both saying yes"** is a line we can say out loud — and it earns the open, the click, the trust. The honesty lock applies to people, not just Atlas facts.

### (d) Alumni & recurring gatherings — the long-term moat

This is the part no incumbent can copy by spending money, because it can only be *grown*. VAWAA sends you home alone; the relationship ends at checkout. MEA's defensible asset isn't its Baja campus — it's the alumni community (a reported 8,000+ across dozens of chapters) that keeps meeting for years, with an annual **Homecoming** as the gravitational centre ([MEA 2026 Homecoming](https://www.santafe.org/listing/mea-|-modern-elder-academy/4384/)). Those figures are MEA's own and directional; what we adapt is the *shape*, applied honestly to where we actually are.

**The honest version of "alumni" (pre-immersion).** We have no immersion alumni yet, so we don't pretend to. Our "alumni" today are **circle regulars** — people who've shown up to practice circles repeatedly. We name and honour *that*: a member who's been to several circles is "a regular of the Lisbon ceramics circle," and that standing is real, earned, and visible on their profile (this is also where the Skill Passport plugs in — see (f)). When the immersion launches in Phase 2, *its* alumni become the deepest tier, but the chapter is the moat whether or not the immersion ever runs.

**Recurring gatherings = the rhythm of belonging.** Three cadences, light to heavy:
- **Monthly:** the practice circle itself (member-run, the heartbeat).
- **Seasonal:** an ET-hosted or host-hosted city gathering per cluster — the rung-4 evening, a few times a year, the moment circles in a city meet each other.
- **Annual (Phase-2 aspiration, honest):** a single Circle gathering — the equivalent of MEA's Homecoming, adapted to our means. Not a conference, not a festival. One real day at one real source where regulars from several cities convene to make something together. We do not build or promise this now; we name it as the north of the rhythm so chapters know they're building toward something.

**Why it's the moat and not just nice:** a chapter that has met monthly for two years has a switching cost no competitor can buy — those are *friendships now*. The relationships outlive any single immersion, any algorithm change, any funded rival's ad budget. And every long-running chapter is a self-sustaining source of engaged subscribers and word-of-mouth referrals — the exact compounding the one metric rewards. The moat is people who keep showing up for each other; we just keep introducing them and getting out of the way.

### (e) Safety, liability, and trust at solo scale

This is the part that, done wrong, ends ET — a solo founder cannot absorb operator liability for strangers meeting in person. So the legal posture is one sentence, repeated everywhere: **ET introduces; ET does not operate.** ET is the matchmaker and the publisher, never the host, the teacher, the venue, or the tour operator. Everything below enforces that line.

> **None of the liability statements below are legal advice.** They are general guidance drawn from platform help-center pages (Meetup), which are not legal authority. Treat every claim in this section as a starting point to take to a lawyer — see the review TODO at the end of the waiver layer — not as settled law.

**The introducer-not-operator stance (load-bearing).** The general principle described in meetup-organizer guidance is that an organiser's exposure tends to turn on whether they *organised* the activity — and that risk concentrates on whoever picked the venue, ran the activity, or controlled the event ([Meetup organizer liability](https://help.meetup.com/hc/en-us/articles/360001674911-Organizer-liability-at-a-Meetup-event), [Meetup Liability and Waivers](https://help.meetup.com/hc/en-us/sections/360000395831-Liability-and-Waivers)). That is general guidance, not a ruling, and it must be confirmed by a lawyer for our jurisdictions — but it shapes the design: ET deliberately controls *none* of it. The volunteer host picks the place and runs the meet; ET only made an introduction and provided a template. This is why the playbook in (b) is written the way it is — every "ET does not give you: money, insurance, space, or instruction" line is a liability firewall, not just an ops convenience.

**The waiver / disclaimer layer (cheap, essential).** Platform terms-of-service typically contain a release by members, but — per the same general guidance — such a release may not bind non-member guests a host might bring, so a direct, activity-specific waiver matters whenever there's any physical risk ([Meetup Liability and Waivers](https://help.meetup.com/hc/en-us/sections/360000395831-Liability-and-Waivers)). Again: directional, to be confirmed with counsel. Our layer:
1. **A clear disclaimer at opt-in and before every introduction:** *"ET introduces people who share a craft. ET does not organise, supervise, insure, or take responsibility for what members do when they meet. You attend at your own risk and judgement."* Plain language, not buried.
2. **A host acknowledgement** the volunteer ticks before getting their first intro: they host as an individual, on their own responsibility, are not ET's agent or employee, and ET provides no insurance or indemnity.
3. **An attendee safety + assumption-of-risk card** before a first meet (the (c) card), with explicit assumption of risk for any physical activity (the kind of "higher-risk or unique activity" case where a waiver is generally advised).
4. **Get all of the above reviewed by a lawyer before any rung-3+ goes live.** This is the one place we do not freelance the legal copy, and it is the review that turns every "general guidance" claim above into something we can rely on. Flag it as a real to-do, not a maybe.

**What ET must NOT take on** (the hard "do not cross" list):
- **Do not handle money for IRL meets.** No paid circles, no ticketed gatherings, no "ET takes a cut of the evening." The moment money flows through ET for an event, ET is arguably an operator. (Phase-2 immersion fees are referral/co-host, arranged **off-site**, and are a different, gated thing.)
- **Do not provide or vouch for venues.** Hosts use spaces they already control; ET never rents, inspects, or certifies a location.
- **Do not supervise minors, alcohol, or high-hazard activity.** Circles are adults-only by default; any genuinely hazardous craft (open water, fire, blades) carries an extra explicit risk acknowledgement and is the host's domain, not ET's.
- **Do not become the safeguarding authority for disputes.** ET sets the safe-space policy and a clear report channel, can remove a member from the Circle, and will — but ET is not the police, the mediator, or the insurer of human behaviour. Be honest about that limit up front.

**Trust signals that double as safety:** the safe-space policy (adapt the FSF/WordPress template — they exist precisely for this), the report-and-remove path, profile longevity (encourage members to keep a real profile before being introduced — a common meetup vetting heuristic ([Meetup safety](https://www.meetup.com/topics/safety/us/))), and the counts-not-names default from (c). Each of these protects members *and* signals seriousness — which, again, deepens the engaged-subscriber relationship the one metric measures. Safety done visibly is a trust feature, not a tax.

### (f) The loop back — IRL → feed → Skill Passport → more sign-ups

The IRL ladder isn't a cost centre we tolerate; it's the **richest content and proof engine we have**, and it closes a self-reinforcing loop straight back into the one metric. Three returns from every real-world moment:

**1. IRL → feed content.** The single thing ET asks every host and attendee for is one photo or one 30–60s clip per meet, tagged **#FromTheCircle**. This is gold for the Daily Drop: it's wordless, it's hands-on-real-material, it ends on a finished object or a moment of competence — it *is* the locked format, made by real members instead of staged. It's also the most honest possible answer to the master test "does it fight the screen or feed it?" — the footage exists *because* people put the screen down and met. We don't pivot the Daily Drop format (stop-doing list); we *feed* it from circles. A steady stream of #FromTheCircle clips means the feed sustains itself without Arnaud filming everything at sea.

**2. IRL → Skill Passport evidence.** The community already has a `prior-experiences` table with an instructor-VERIFIED flag, and the future build target is verifiable skill credentials. The practice circle is where self-declared becomes *witnessed*. When a member attends a circle, the **host can confirm attendance** (a light, honest tap — "yes, they were here and they threw a pot"), and that confirmation becomes a real entry on the member's Skill Passport: *"3 sessions · Lisbon ceramics circle · host-confirmed."* Not a fabricated badge — a witnessed fact, which honours the honesty lock (verified against a real human who was there, exactly like Atlas facts are verified against the source). This is the credential layer made trustworthy *by* the IRL layer. It's also the bridge to Phase-2: when the immersion gate opens, a member's circle history is the proof they're ready — "earned, not bought," recorded.

**3. ## Part 8 — The Tram Rail: intelligent, honest automation

A tram runs fast because it's locked to a rail. It can't wander into the crowd. That's the whole idea here. Automation should carry the dull weight — file-shuffling, scheduling, first drafts, reminders — so Arnaud's scarce hours go to the two things no machine can fake: **verifying a fact** and **answering a human warmly**. Every minute the rail saves has exactly one job: more engaged Circle subscribers. If a piece of automation doesn't feed that metric, or it touches a fact or a person's trust, it doesn't get full speed. It gets a human at the controls.

The discipline that makes this safe is already in the codebase. `ig-lint.py` has a `BANNED` list and a credential-keyword gate. `build-daily-drop.py` refuses banned words and prices at generation time. `circle-welcome / circle-broadcast / circle-unsubscribe` already send mail through Resend. We are not building an empire. We are wiring the gaps and putting a linter in front of the door.

> **A note on the figures and policy claims below.** The platform rules, governance patterns, and "2026 best-practice" splits cited here are drawn from third-party 2026 analyses and provider help-pages — they are directional, not gospel, and they shift. Verify the load-bearing ones (platform disclosure rules, model id/pricing) against the primary source at build time, and track real performance in the Studio Metrics tab rather than betting the format on someone's blog number.

### (a) The Automation Map — one level per surface

Three levels, and the rule for choosing is blunt: **anything factual or trust-bearing can never be full-auto.** Atlas facts, who-teaches-what, credentials — a machine may *draft* the claim, but a human asserts it. That's the HONESTY LOCK rendered as plumbing.

| Surface | Level | Why this level |
|---|---|---|
| **Atlas fact-keeping** (school cards, masters, courses, confidence tags) | **Human-only** | This *is* the moat. Every fact is hand-verified against the school's official source. A model may pre-fill a "provisional, verifying" row, but Arnaud confirms before the confidence tag flips. Never auto-assert who teaches what. |
| **/atlas page builds** (the 424 static SEO pages) | **Full-auto** | `build-atlas-pages.py` renders verified data into HTML. The *facts* were human-gated upstream; the *rendering* is deterministic and safe to run on every deploy. Mechanical, not editorial. |
| **Daily-Drop scheduling** (calendar, which atlas page each clip points at) | **Full-auto** | `build-daily-drop.py` already selects by communityRank, round-robins the 4 cores, no repeats, and refuses banned words. The selection is rules-based, so let it run. |
| **Daily-Drop clip itself** (the 30-60s wordless process video) | **Human-only** | The clip is the soul. Real hands, real material, shot or sourced by Arnaud. Never AI-generated footage — that fails the master test ("fight the screen or feed it?") and would be a lie about "at the source." |
| **Caption / hook / idea generation** | **Draft-then-approve** | A model drafts the caption from the locked recipe (superlative hook + atlas 'why' + community/season + one-line "what we are" + the one URL + 4-5 hashtags). `ig-lint.py` gates it. Arnaud reads it before it posts. |
| **Journal drafting** (the essay pillar) | **Draft-then-approve** | A model can assemble a cited outline and a rough draft from verified sources. But the Journal is "science-and-stories" with citations — every claim needs a human check, and the voice must be founder-led, so Arnaud rewrites and signs it. |

| **Outreach follow-ups** | **Draft-then-approve** | `find-followups.py` + `outreach-digest.py` already surface who's overdue. The *reminder* is auto; the *message* is human-approved. A follow-up that sounds like a bot ends the relationship. |

A human decides removals and bans. Never auto-delete a member's post on a model's say-so; never auto-generate "engagement." |

It is issued only after a human (Arnaud or a vetted instructor) verifies — exactly like the existing instructor-VERIFIED flag on prior-experiences. A model never mints a badge. |
| **Metrics** (Plausible + Circle open/click + outreach digest) | **Full-auto** | Reading and summarising numbers is safe and high-leverage. Auto-pull into the Studio Metrics tab. The *interpretation* and any decision stays human. |

### (c) Honesty guardrails — what makes the automation safe

These are non-negotiable. They are the HONESTY LOCK as enforced rules, not good intentions.

1. **Human-in-the-loop on everything factual or trust-bearing.** Atlas facts, credentials, and who-teaches-what are *never* auto-asserted. A model may draft; a human asserts. If a fact can't be verified against the school's official source, it ships as **"provisional, verifying"** with a low-confidence tag, never as fact. This is the "provisional, verifying" discipline written into the workflow, not just the copy.
2. **No fake engagement, ever.** No bots, no sockpuppets, no purchased followers, no AI-generated "members" in the Circle or the feed, no auto-liking. Community-RANK is "best chance to find your cohort," not reputation theatre. Faked engagement would corrupt THE ONE METRIC (engaged subscribers) at its root — you'd be optimising a lie.
3. **AI-assistance disclosure norms.** The clip is real footage, always — so there's nothing synthetic to disclose there, which is the honest position and, per current 2026 platform guidance, the compliant one (the read at the time of writing is that TikTok/Meta require disclosure only for *synthetic or significantly AI-altered realistic* media, with real process footage exempt — but treat this as a moving rule and re-verify against the platforms' own policy pages before relying on it: [platform policies summary](https://susit.ai/blog/platform-policies-ai-content-2026); [TikTok synthetic-media rules](https://www.auditsocials.com/blog/tiktok-ai-content-disclosure-rules-2026)). Where a model *did* draft copy that a human edited, that reads as standard assisted writing under current norms — but the rule we hold is simpler and stricter than any platform's: **we never present a machine-made claim as a verified human one.** No AI-generated faces, voices, or fabricated "students." If we ever publish anything genuinely synthetic, we label it plainly. Trust is the product ([FTC disclosure guidance](https://humanadsai.com/blog/ftc-ai-generated-content-disclosure); [creator authenticity checklist](https://blog.crescitaly.com/ai-creator-authenticity-2026-disclosure-checklist-social-growth/)).
4. **The brand-lock / banned-word linter as a hard gate before anything posts.** Extend `ig-lint.py` so *nothing reaches a public surface without passing it.* Concretely:
   - Keep the existing `BANNED` list; add the canon's public-surface bans — `book`, `enroll`, `checkout`, `Quest`, `XP/level/badge` (as gamification), `price/$`, and "choose your depth" as a pricing ladder.
   - Add a positive check: the verb must be **introduce / connect / bridge**, never sell / book / enroll.
   - Add a **fact-claim flag**: if a draft asserts a specific master, school, or credential, the linter requires a `verified: true` field in frontmatter (sourced) or it fails. A claim with no source can't pass.
   - Wire it as a pre-commit / pre-publish step in `build-daily-drop.py`, the Journal build, and the `circle-broadcast` send path. Exit 1 = no post. The linter is the tram's rail-guard: copy cannot derail into hype, selling, or an unsourced fact.

The principle 2026 governance write-ups converge on: automation is safe only with **audit trails and a human publish gate** (directional, per [cflow on 2026 trends](https://www.cflowapps.com/ai-workflow-automation-trends/)). The linter *is* our audit trail — every pass/fail is logged.

### (d) The Model-Refresh Loop — keep the rail modern without breaking the locks

Models improve fast; the rail should ride the best available without ever loosening a lock. **Default to the latest, most capable model when building any AI feature** — for ET's drafting (captions, Journal outlines, outreach notes) that means the current frontier Claude model rather than an older or cheaper tier, because the cost is trivial against a solo founder's hours and quality directly feeds open/reply rates. Verify the exact current model id and pricing against Anthropic's docs at build time — never hardcode a model id or price from memory into a script or this doc.

**The ritual — "Tram Service" — runs monthly (last Sunday, ~30 min), recorded in the Studio Plan tab under a `MODEL-REFRESH` note and mirrored to the MEMORY project file:**

Checklist:
- [ ] **What's new?** Check for a newer/more capable model from the providers we use (Claude first). Note the current best model id + pricing from the provider's own docs.
- [ ] **Re-bench the drafters.** Run the same three real prompts (one caption, one outreach note, one Journal paragraph) through the current model vs. the new one. Does the new one need fewer edits? Fewer linter fails?
- [ ] **Cost check.** Is the new model affordable at our volume (a few dozen drafts/week)? Almost always yes — quality wins.
- [ ] **Locks still hold?** After switching, run the full `ig-lint.py` suite on the new outputs. If the new model trips the linter more, that's a regression — fix the prompt or hold.
- [ ] **One change at a time.** Swap the model behind *one* surface (e.g. captions) first, watch one week of linter pass-rate and edit-time, then roll to the others. Never swap everything blind.
- [ ] **New tool scan.** Any new automation tool worth wiring (better scheduler, better transcription for clip captions)? Only adopt if it saves real founder-minutes and respects the offline-tolerance rule.
- [ ] **Record it.** Date, model id, what changed, edit-time delta, linter pass-rate. One line in the Studio Plan note so the next refresh has a baseline.

The locks (design, voice, banned words, honesty, no-selling) are **upstream of the model.** The model is a swappable engine; the rail-guard (`ig-lint.py`) doesn't care which engine drafted the text. That's what lets you adopt a better model in minutes without risking the brand.

### (e) Pragmatic build order for a solo founder at sea (offline-tolerant)

Everything here is **draft-offline, send-when-connected** — the founder is often at sea, so generation happens locally (scripts + cached data + the model), and only the *send* steps need a connection. Time saved is per week, the unit that matters.

1. **Extend `ig-lint.py` into the universal gate** (½ day build). *Saves: nothing directly — it's the seatbelt that makes everything below safe to automate.* Do this first; it's the rail.
2. **Caption drafter on `build-daily-drop.py` → Studio Daily-Drop tab** (½ day). Generates a week of lint-passed caption drafts in one offline run; Arnaud approves in batches. *Saves ~30-40 min/week.*
3. **Metrics auto-pull → Studio Metrics tab** (½ day). One weekly snapshot of THE ONE METRIC (engaged subs, open/click) + outreach digest. *Saves ~20 min/week and keeps focus on the right number.*
4. **Outreach drafter on `next-outreach-batch.py` + `find-followups.py`** (1 day). Pre-drafted, personalised, lint-passed notes queued for approval. *Saves ~60-90 min/week once outreach is regular.*
5. **Journal draft-assist** (½ day prompt work, no new infra). Cited outline + rough draft from verified sources into the Articles tab. *Saves ~2-3 hrs per essay* — but the rewrite stays human.
6. **Finish `social-publish` / `ig_publish.py`** (gated on IG Business + Meta token, not on code). Closes approved-caption → posted across all three platforms. *Saves ~15-20 min/day of manual cross-posting.*

**Phase-gated (do NOT build now):** the skills-only feed's moderation automation, credential issuance, and the linktree/IRL mechanisms are later-moves — they only matter once the Circle is large enough to need them (toward the PHASE-2 GATE). Building moderation before there's a feed, or credential-minting before there are credentials, is gold-plating. The rail carries today's load: get the clip out, the caption right, the issue sent, the outreach warm, the number visible. That's what grows the one metric.

The whole rail in a sentence: **let machines render, schedule, remind, and draft — let Arnaud verify and connect — and put one linter in front of every door.** Fast where it's safe, human where it counts.

## Part 9 — Sequencing & the focus filter

The nine Parts are a menu, not a to-do list. If we chase all of them at once we lose focus, and losing focus is the one failure this whole document exists to prevent. So here is the order — what we do NOW, what comes NEXT, and what stays locked behind the 8-gate until the numbers earn it. Everything below is judged by one question: does it move **engaged Circle subscribers**? If a move can't be traced to that number within a step or two, it waits.

### NOW (the next 90 days — the only fight)

These run in parallel because they are one loop: attention in, sign-ups out.

- **The Daily Drop, every day, format locked** (Part 2). The wordless process clip earns the eyeball. It is the top of the funnel; nothing else works without it. Do not pivot the format pre-day-90.
- **Every public surface routes to one Atlas URL or the Circle** (Parts 1, 2). One destination per piece. Caption carries the "what we are" line and a single link. *Maps to the metric:* every view that clicks through is a sign-up candidate.
- **The relationship program, one discipline at a time** (Part 3). Feature schools, send them warm strangers, earn the repost. We introduce, we don't sell. *Maps to the metric:* every school share puts our line and our link in front of exactly the people most likely to open and click.
- **Studio Metrics is the scoreboard** (Parts 2, 7, 8). Read our own per-stream data weekly. Treat every external figure as a hypothesis to confirm here, never as a law. *Maps to the metric:* we steer the format toward what actually produces engaged subs.

### NEXT (start small while NOW is running — deepen retention, don't replace the funnel)

These make the people we already have open more, reply more, refer more. They layer on; they never pull effort off the Daily Drop.

- **The Skill Passport — profile first** (Part 5). Turn the read-only crew card at `/crew` into one public page a person is proud to put in their bio. Build the profile now; build the credential model second and slower. The passport's only job is to make the honesty visible. *Maps to the metric:* a member proud of their page shares it — and a shared page is a warm new sign-up.
- **The Community room — skills-only, signal over dopamine** (Part 4). A room where people chasing the same fire show each other their hands at work, not another feed. Small and active beats big and empty. *Maps to the metric:* a member who replies and refers is the deepest kind of engaged sub.
- **The Tram Rail — wire the gaps** (Part 8). Let automation carry the dull weight so Arnaud's scarce hours go to the two things no machine can fake: verifying a fact and answering a human warmly. Keep the linter in front of the door. *Maps to the metric:* every hour the rail saves is an hour spent producing more engaged subs.

### PHASE-2-GATED (do not start until all 8 are true)

- **IRL — the first off-site connection** (Part 6). A click becomes a coffee, a coffee a practice circle. The soft refundable-deposit immersion test (gate 7) lives here. IRL is not a new product; it is the deepest expression of the one metric.
- **Curated partner immersions — the first money** (Parts 3, 7). Referral or co-host fee, arranged off-site, never an on-site transaction, only after the 8-gate. One immersion at a time, and only if it won't starve Atlas + Circle.

### The month-6 checkpoint (kill / persevere)

At day 180, look at the real numbers in Studio Metrics — not the third-party figures, ours. Persevere if the trend toward the 8-gate is real: organic non-branded traffic climbing toward "several hundred a month, 3+ months sustained," Circle subs growing with 40%+ open and 6–9% click, and the first unprompted "can you connect me?" notes landing in the inbox. If the metric is flat after six honest months of the locked format and one-discipline-at-a-time outreach, do not add features and do not pivot the video — change the *angle and the hook*, the cheapest variable, and run another clean stretch. We kill effort that doesn't move the one number; we do not kill the line.

### Before you ship anything

Run the Focus Filter, then remember the **STOP-DOING list** above — most of the ways we can lose are things we already decided not to do. And remember the first law, the law under every other line in this document: **never lose focus.** Open on the payoff, one idea per piece, re-hook, close on one thing. Every line earns the next, or it goes.
